2This article studies the cartesian conception of freedom, first in its metaphysical essence, as resolution of the singular ego to enlightened freedom, both in thought and in action, and then in its moral and political consequences. It shows that Descartes announces a heroic conception of freedom, by virtue of the nobility of the free will present in all men, which implies an interiorization and a universalization of the aristocratic ideal, in a morality of generosity and charity in friendship. It sketches from there a cartesian policy liable in the person of the individual and in that of the prince, as the union of infinite and free wills, and as the life which opens on the most heroic of human actions, in a world which places importance on being a gentleman.

1L'article analyse la compréhension biranienne de la volonté chez Descartes. Pour Biran, Descartes aurait confondu le désir et la volonté, c'est-à-dire la passivité et l'activité. Pour Biran, l'infinité de la volonté n'est en réalité que celle d'une aspiration sans limite déterminée.

2The item analyses Biran's understanding of the cartesian concept of will. According to him, Descartes would have mistaken desire forwill, passivity with activity. Condillac is first criticized by Biran, but Malebranche too, because through occasionalism the evidence of effort and power is denied. Concerning Descartes, Biran puts forward that cartesian doubt cannot be assumed because of embodiment. He also underlines that choice (requiring both intellect and will) has no link with real will, because of the passivity of cartesian intellect. According to Biran, the infinity of will is, in fact, a mere boundless and vague aspiration.

2Thinking by oneself: in the preliminary chapter of the second Democracy in America, Tocqueville questions such a motto from the Moderns which is also their philosophical method. Through the American example, i.e. the very type of democratic anthropology, he stresses ambivalence and questions the promises of cartesian philosophy : if individual reason is freed from tradition and prejudices, will everyone find in his own self the means of a reasoned exercize of judgement ? Reversely the void of minds may find itself revealed, dangerously filled as it might by the answers from the public opinion, i. e. sovereign within democracy. Showing how intellectual individualism is at the origin of a new kind of alienation, Tocqueville inscribes doubt in the heart of liberal optimism right at a time when official liberalism (Cousin, Guizot) acknowledges cartesianism and worships in it the true French spirit.
