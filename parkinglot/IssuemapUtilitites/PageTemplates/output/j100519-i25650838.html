1In the decade of the 1940s, the painter and political activist David Alfaro Siqueiros developed a preoccupation with the figure of Cuauhtémoc, last of the Aztec Emperors. His many portrayals of the ruler operated on multiple levels of meaning and brought together disparate strands of his radical thought. The treatment of the subject in murals, easel paintings, prints and drawings provided a means for the artist to express his political beliefs and helped him formulate his aesthetic positions. Siqueiros also identified psychologically with the historical figure, and the example of Cuauhtémoc's undaunted resistance to the Spanish Conquest inspired him to assert his own convictions, as exemplified in his controversial book of 1945, No hay más ruta que la nuestra.
2This article situates the Cuauhtémoc imagery within Siqueiros's political activity, with reference to his anti-Fascist initiatives, his association with the Labor movement and Communist Party, and his problematic relationship with the regimes of Presidents Ávila Camacho and Alemán. In this way, we see that Siqueiros's own struggles in theory and practice reflect the predicaments of the radical Left at mid-century.
